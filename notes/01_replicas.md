# entanglement has a geometric origin

### (2+1)D & (1+1)D

In (1+1)D, the flux tube has no transverse direction, so it is essentially a one-dimensional object without an intrinsic width.

```
(1+1)D

q ================= q̄
```

Since there is no transverse degree of freedom, the internal structure of the flux tube is relatively simple.

In (2+1)D, however, an additional transverse spatial dimension becomes available.

```
(2+1)D

        ↑ y
        │
q ================= q̄
        │
        ↓
```

The color flux can fluctuate and spread in the transverse direction, giving the flux tube a finite width and richer internal dynamics.

Consequently, the Flux Tube Entanglement (FTE) may exhibit new, non-trivial behavior that is absent in (1+1)D.

Future studies could investigate this by introducing multiple entangling regions (slabs) between the quark and antiquark, allowing one to probe how entanglement depends on the geometry of the flux tube.

**Key idea**

- (1+1)D: flux tube ≈ one-dimensional string
- no transverse fluctuations
- no intrinsic width
- relatively simple entanglement structure

↓

- (2+1)D: transverse fluctuations appear
- finite flux-tube width
- richer internal structure
- potentially richer entanglement physics
